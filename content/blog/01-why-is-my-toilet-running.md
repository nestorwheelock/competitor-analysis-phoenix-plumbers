## Common Causes of a Running Toilet

### 1. Worn or Damaged Flapper

**The most common cause of running toilets.**

The flapper is the rubber seal at the bottom of your tank. When you flush, it lifts to let water into the bowl, then settles back to create a watertight seal. Over time, flappers:
- Deteriorate and become stiff
- Warp from chlorine and hard water
- Accumulate mineral deposits
- Develop cracks or tears

**How to check:** Add a few drops of food coloring to the tank (don't flush). Wait 15-20 minutes. If color appears in the bowl, your flapper isn't sealing.

**DIY fix:** Flappers are inexpensive ($5-15) and relatively easy to replace:
1. Turn off the water supply
2. Flush to empty the tank
3. Unhook the old flapper from the overflow tube and disconnect the chain
4. Install the new flapper and connect the chain
5. Turn the water back on and test

### 2. Float Problems

The float tells the fill valve when to stop filling. If it's set too high, water rises above the overflow tube and constantly drains. If the float is damaged or waterlogged, it may not rise properly.

**Types of floats:**
- **Ball float** â€“ Ball on an arm connected to the fill valve
- **Cup float** â€“ Cylinder that moves up and down on the fill valve

**How to check:** Remove the tank lid and flush. Watch where the water level stops. It should be about 1 inch below the overflow tube.

**DIY fix:**
- For ball floats: Bend the arm down slightly to lower the water level
- For cup floats: Adjust the screw or clip on the side to lower the float
- If the float is waterlogged: Replace it

### 3. Fill Valve Issues

The fill valve controls water flow into the tank. When it fails, it may run continuously or cycle on and off.

**Signs of fill valve problems:**
- Constant hissing sound
- Water spraying inside tank
- Very slow tank refill
- Inconsistent water levels

**DIY fix:** Fill valves can be replaced ($15-30), but it requires more skill than flapper replacement. If you're comfortable with DIY plumbing:
1. Turn off water and flush
2. Disconnect the water supply line
3. Remove the old fill valve
4. Install the new valve per instructions
5. Reconnect and test

### 4. Chain Issues

The chain connecting the flush handle to the flapper needs the right amount of slack:
- **Too much slack:** The flapper doesn't lift fully
- **Too little slack:** The flapper can't seat properly

**DIY fix:** Adjust the chain to have about 1/2 inch of slack when the flapper is seated.

### 5. Overflow Tube Problems

If the overflow tube is cracked or too short, water constantly drains into the bowl.

**DIY fix:** The overflow tube is typically part of the flush valve assembly, requiring more extensive replacement. This is often better left to a professional.

### 6. Flush Valve Seal

The flush valve seal is where the flapper sits. If it's corroded, warped, or has mineral buildup, even a new flapper won't seal properly.

**DIY fix:** Try cleaning the seal with an abrasive pad. If that doesn't work, the flush valve may need replacementâ€”a job for most professionals.

## Step-by-Step Troubleshooting Guide

### Step 1: Listen and Observe

- **Constant running:** Usually flapper or float set too high
- **Cycling on/off (ghost flushing):** Usually a slow leak past the flapper
- **Hissing:** Usually fill valve issue

### Step 2: Check the Flapper

- Do the food coloring test
- Visually inspect for damage
- Feel for stiffness or warping
- Check that the chain has proper slack

### Step 3: Check the Float

- Flush and watch the water level
- Water should stop 1" below overflow tube
- Adjust float if necessary
- Check for waterlogged float

### Step 4: Check the Fill Valve

- Listen for hissing at the fill valve
- Watch for spraying water
- Check that it shuts off completely

## When to Call a Professional

While many running toilet issues are DIY-friendly, some situations call for professional help:

### Call a plumber if:
- You've replaced the flapper and the problem persists
- The flush valve or overflow tube needs replacement
- You're not comfortable working with plumbing
- The toilet has other issues (leaking at base, rocking, etc.)
- You've tried troubleshooting without success

### A professional can:
- Diagnose problems accurately
- Replace complete internal mechanisms efficiently
- Identify if the toilet should be replaced entirely
- Ensure repairs are done correctly

## Preventing Future Problems

### Regular Maintenance
- Replace flappers every 3-5 years proactively
- Check tank components annually
- Clean mineral deposits periodically

### Water Quality Considerations
Phoenix's hard water accelerates component wear. Consider:
- Water softener to reduce mineral buildup
- More frequent flapper replacement
- Periodic cleaning of tank components

### Quality Replacement Parts
When replacing parts, choose quality:
- Avoid ultra-cheap universal flappers
- Match parts to your toilet brand/model
- Consider korky or Fluidmaster brands
