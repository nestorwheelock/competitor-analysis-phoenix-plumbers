## Kitchen Drain: The Grease Trap

The kitchen drain is the most commonly clogged drain in most homes, and the cause is almost always the same: grease, oil, and food particles.

### How Kitchen Clogs Form

1. Hot grease goes down the drain (seems liquid enough)
2. As it cools, it congeals on pipe walls
3. Food particles stick to the grease
4. Layer by layer, the clog builds
5. Eventually, water can't pass

### What NOT to Put Down the Kitchen Drain

**Cooking grease and oil:** This includes:
- Bacon grease
- Vegetable oil after frying
- Butter and margarine
- Salad dressing
- Meat drippings
- Coconut oil

**Even "small amounts" add up.** A tablespoon of grease today, a tablespoon tomorrowâ€”over months and years, that's pounds of grease coating your pipes.

**Foods that clog (even with a garbage disposal):**
- Coffee grounds (they don't break down)
- Eggshells (create gritty buildup)
- Pasta and rice (expand with water)
- Potato peels (starchy paste)
- Fibrous vegetables (celery, asparagus, artichokes)
- Bones (disposal can't fully grind them)
- Fruit pits

### Kitchen Prevention Tips

**Handle grease properly:**
1. Let grease cool and solidify
2. Scrape into a container (coffee can, jar, etc.)
3. Dispose with regular trash
4. For small amounts, wipe pans with paper towel before washing

**Use a drain strainer:**
- Catches food particles before they enter the drain
- Empty into trash, not the disposal
- Cost: $5-$15
- Return on investment: Potentially hundreds in avoided service calls

**Run water with the disposal:**
- Run cold water before, during, and after using the disposal
- Cold water keeps grease solid so it moves through (hot water melts it, then it resolidifies in pipes)
- Run disposal completely clear before stopping water

**Monthly maintenance:**
- Pour boiling water down the drain (not if you have PVC pipesâ€”use very hot tap water instead)
- Follow with dish soap
- This helps dissolve minor grease accumulation

## Bathroom Sink: Soap Scum and Whiskers

Bathroom sink clogs are usually caused by soap scum, toothpaste, and hair (especially from shaving).

### How Bathroom Sink Clogs Form

1. Soap and toothpaste create a sticky coating
2. Hair catches in the drain stopper
3. More soap and paste stick to the hair
4. The glob grows and restricts flow

### Bathroom Sink Prevention Tips

**Clean the stopper regularly:**
- Most pop-up stoppers lift out or unscrew
- Remove and clean every 2-4 weeks
- You'll be disgusted by what accumulatesâ€”that's the point

**Don't rinse hair down the drain:**
- After shaving, wipe sink with paper towel before rinsing
- Clean brushes and combs over a trash can, not the sink

**Use a drain cover:**
- Mesh drain catches prevent hair from entering
- Especially important if you shave at the sink

**Monthly flush:**
- Run hot water for 30 seconds
- Helps move soap residue through pipes

## Shower and Bathtub: Hair Central

Hair is the primary cause of shower and bathtub clogsâ€”and the average person sheds 50-100 hairs per day. Much of that happens in the shower.

### How Shower Clogs Form

1. Hair washes off and heads toward drain
2. Soap and conditioner help hair clump together
3. Hair catches on drain cover, stopper, or pipe walls
4. The mass grows with each shower
5. Eventually, water won't drain

### Shower and Tub Prevention Tips

**Install a hair catcher:**
- Silicone drain covers: Flat, fit over most drains, easy to clean ($5-$10)
- Mesh strainers: Drop into drain opening ($3-$8)
- TubShroom or similar: Fits inside the drain, catches hair before it goes down ($10-$15)

These simple devices catch hair before it enters your pipes. Clean them after each showerâ€”takes 5 seconds.

**Remove visible hair:**
- Before showering, wipe visible hair from the drain cover
- After showering, collect hair that accumulated

**Monthly maintenance:**
- Remove the drain cover and pull out any hair accumulation (needle-nose pliers help)
- Use a hair clog tool (plastic barbed strip) to grab hair in the first few inches of drain

**What about chemical drain cleaners?**
- They work poorly on hair clogs
- Harsh chemicals can damage pipes
- Better prevention: Don't let hair go down the drain in the first place

## Toilet: Only the Three Ps

Toilets are designed to handle human waste and toilet paper. That's it. Not:

- "Flushable" wipes (they're not really flushableâ€”they don't break down)
- Feminine hygiene products
- Cotton swabs or cotton balls
- Dental floss
- Hair
- Diapers
- Paper towels or tissues
- Cat litter (even "flushable" types)
- Medication (also an environmental issue)

### The "Flushable" Wipe Problem

Flushable wipes are plumbing's most deceptive product. They may flush down your toilet, but they don't disintegrate like toilet paper. They accumulate in pipes, combine with other debris, and create stubborn clogsâ€”often deep in the line where they're expensive to clear.

**The test:** Drop a sheet of toilet paper in water and a "flushable" wipe. Agitate both. The toilet paper disintegrates. The wipe stays intact. That tells you everything you need to know.

### Toilet Prevention Tips

**Only flush the three Ps:**
- Pee
- Poop
- (toilet) Paper

Everything else goes in the trashâ€”even if the package says "flushable."

**Use reasonable amounts of toilet paper:**
- Excessive toilet paper (especially cheap, thick varieties) can cause clogs
- Multiple smaller flushes are better than one massive one

**Keep a quality plunger nearby:**
- Flange plunger (with extended rubber flap) works better than cup plunger
- Address slow flushes immediatelyâ€”they get worse

## Laundry: Lint and Debris

Washing machine drains handle a surprising amount of debris: lint, fabric fibers, pet hair, tissues left in pockets, and more.

### Laundry Drain Prevention

**Use a lint trap on the discharge hose:**
- Mesh lint traps catch fibers before they enter the drain
- Cost: $5-$10
- Replace when full or damaged

**Check pockets before washing:**
- Tissues, receipts, and small items create debris
- Pet owners: Brush pets before washing their bedding

**Clean inlet screens:**
- Washing machines have small screens where water enters
- Mineral deposits and debris can clog these
- Check and clean annually

## Whole-House Prevention

### Annual Drain Maintenance

Even with good habits, minor buildup occurs. Annual maintenance prevents accumulation from becoming problematic.

**DIY maintenance:**
- Flush all drains with hot water monthly
- Clean all drain stoppers and strainers monthly
- Use enzyme-based drain cleaners quarterly (not chemical cleaners)

**Professional maintenance:**
- Annual drain cleaning for problem-prone drains
- Camera inspection if you have recurring issues
- Mainline cleaning every 1-2 years for homes with trees near sewer lines

### The Role of Trees

If you have trees near your sewer line, roots can infiltrate pipes through joints and cracks. This isn't something homeowners can prevent through behaviorâ€”it requires:

- Professional root treatment
- Periodic mechanical root cutting
- Possible pipe repair or replacement

Annual camera inspection identifies root intrusion before it becomes a crisis.

### Know Your Cleanouts

Your plumbing system has cleanout access pointsâ€”usually capped white pipes near the foundation or in the yard. These allow plumbers to access your drain lines without entering through fixtures.

Know where yours are. If you need drain cleaning, accessible cleanouts speed the service and reduce cost.

## When to Call a Professional

### DIY Appropriate

- Single slow drain (try plunging or drain snake)
- Hair visible at drain opening (remove it)
- Minor kitchen slowdown (hot water and soap flush)

### Professional Recommended

- Multiple drains slow simultaneously (mainline issue)
- Recurring clogs in the same location
- Sewage smell from drains
- Gurgling sounds from drains
- Complete blockage that plunging doesn't clear
- Any main sewer line backup

### Emergency

- Sewage backing up into the house
- Water flooding from drains
- Multiple fixtures affected simultaneously

## The Bottom Line

Most drain clogs are preventable with simple habits:

1. **Kitchen:** Keep grease out of the drain, use a strainer
2. **Bathroom sink:** Clean the stopper regularly, don't rinse hair down
3. **Shower/tub:** Use a hair catcher, clean it after each use
4. **Toilet:** Only flush the three Ps
5. **Laundry:** Use a lint trap on the discharge hose

These small daily actions save significant money and frustration over time. Prevention always costs less than repair.
